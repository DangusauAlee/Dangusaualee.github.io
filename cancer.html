Methodology

The methodology employed in this project revolves around two key components: the development of a Cancer Monitoring Dashboard using Power BI and the implementation of a machine learning algorithm utilizing the Decision Tree Classifier from the Scikit-Learn library in Python. The following sections outline the step-by-step approach undertaken to achieve the project's objectives.
1. Data Collection and Preprocessing:
Comprehensive cancer patient data was collected, encompassing medical records, diagnostic reports, appointment histories, and patient demographics.
Data preprocessing involved cleaning, handling missing values, and standardizing formats to ensure the dataset's quality and consistency.
Features of interest, including patient age, medical history, diagnostic results, and risk factors, were identified and extracted.
2. Dashboard Development using Power BI:
The Cancer Monitoring Dashboard was designed using the Power BI platform, aiming to provide a user-friendly and informative interface for hospital staff.
Visualizations, including bar charts, Pie Charts, and Histograms, were employed to convey key insights such as patient demographics, appointment queues, and risk distributions.
Interactive filters and drill-down functionalities were integrated to allow users to explore data and trends dynamically.
3. Feature Engineering and Selection:
Relevant features were selected from the preprocessed dataset, considering their potential impact on patient prioritization.
Feature engineering techniques were applied to derive additional insights, such as risk scores or composite indicators, to enhance the model's predictive capabilities.
4. Machine Learning Model Development:
A Decision Tree Classifier, a powerful and interpretable machine learning algorithm, was chosen for patient prioritization.
The dataset was split into training and testing sets to facilitate model evaluation and validation.
The Decision Tree Classifier was trained on the training set, utilizing features to predict patient priority levels.
5. Model Evaluation and Tuning:
The model's performance was assessed using metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score.
Hyperparameter tuning was performed to optimize the model's parameters and improve its predictive accuracy.

The accuracy of the model being 0.9082 indicates that it performs well in predicting patient priority across the entire dataset. However, given the high class imbalance and the critical nature of the model, focusing on specific metrics like False Positives (FP) becomes crucial to ensure that high-priority patients receive the appropriate attention.
Let's interpret the confusion matrix and its implications:

Confusion Matrix:
True Negatives (TN): 51
False Positives (FP): 49
False Negatives (FN): 10
True Positives (TP): 620
Interpretation:
True Negatives (TN): The model correctly predicted 51 instances as low priority.
False Positives (FP): The model incorrectly predicted 49 instances as high priority when they were actually low priority. This is a critical concern since it may result in low-priority patients receiving unnecessary urgent attention.
False Negatives (FN): The model incorrectly predicted 10 instances as low priority when they were actually high priority. This is also a concern as it means some high-priority patients were not properly identified.
True Positives (TP): The model correctly predicted 620 instances as high priority.

In summary, while the model's accuracy is commendable, the challenge lies in optimizing the balance between high precision and recall, particularly with a focus on minimizing False Positives. Addressing the class imbalance and refining the model's thresholds or techniques might lead to a more balanced outcome that prioritizes patient care effectively while reducing the risk of unnecessary interventions for low-priority patients.
